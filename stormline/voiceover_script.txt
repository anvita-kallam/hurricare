When a hurricane makes landfall, relief coordinators have hours to allocate billions of dollars across affected regions — splitting funding between shelter, food, health, water, logistics, and recovery, with incomplete data and no margin for error. There is currently no system that lets them rehearse these decisions before they have to make them for real.

The core issue is structural. Decades of disaster response data — severity assessments, population displacement figures, funding allocations, coverage outcomes — exist across fragmented sources. No coordinator can synthesize it all under time pressure. The result: the same funding gaps repeat across disasters, and lessons from past responses never reach the next response team in a usable form.

HurriCare consolidates this data into a single analytical platform and turns every historical disaster into a playable simulation. Coordinators practice real allocation decisions using real constraints, then receive a quantitative comparison of their performance.

The workflow is direct. Select a historical hurricane from a 3D globe. The system generates a full operational briefing — storm trajectory, wind speed progression, region-by-region severity indices, total population affected — reconstructed from actual humanitarian records.

From there, you allocate. You receive the documented relief budget and a response window, then distribute funding across affected regions and six humanitarian clusters. The budget is zero-sum — every dollar routed to one cluster in one region is unavailable elsewhere. The constraints mirror real operational tradeoffs.

Once submitted, the system runs your plan through a machine learning pipeline and returns a three-way comparison: your allocation, an ML-optimized distribution that maximizes projected coverage, and the actual historical allocation — what was really spent.

This comparison is the analytical core of the platform. It surfaces exactly where a user's plan diverges from the mathematical optimum, and where the real-world response diverged from both. Chronic underfunding patterns, regional blind spots, and cluster-level gaps that historically required months of post-crisis analysis become visible immediately.

A global funding disparity layer maps where relief funding has systematically fallen short of estimated need across the full dataset — exposing structural gaps at the institutional level.

The output is measurable. Coordinators using HurriCare enter real crises having already executed these allocation decisions under realistic constraints, with a quantified understanding of where response systems consistently fail. The platform does not replace human judgment — it ensures those judgments are informed by the full weight of historical evidence, not made cold.
