When a hurricane hits, relief coordinators have hours to decide how billions of dollars get split across entire regions. Who gets medical aid first. Which areas get shelter funding. How much goes to food versus water versus logistics. They make these calls with incomplete data, and there is no way to practice.

HurriCare changes that. It is a simulation platform built on real historical disaster data — every hurricane, every funding record, every coverage outcome from the past several decades — all in one place.

Here is how it works.

You start by browsing a 3D globe of historical hurricanes. Pick one — say, Hurricane Matthew — and the system drops you into a full cinematic briefing: the storm's track across the map, wind speeds climbing through categories, region-by-region severity, and exactly how many people were affected.

Then you get the situation assessment. Population in need by region. Severity breakdowns. Coverage gaps. Everything a real coordinator would see in a crisis room, pulled from actual data.

Now you make the hard decisions. You are given the real relief budget and a response window, and you allocate funding across regions and humanitarian clusters — shelter, food, health, water, logistics, recovery. Every dollar you assign to one area is a dollar you cannot spend somewhere else. The tradeoffs are real because the numbers are real.

Once you lock in your plan, the system runs it through a machine learning pipeline and compares your allocation against two benchmarks: an ML-optimized plan that calculates the mathematically ideal distribution, and the actual historical response — what really happened.

That three-way comparison is the core of HurriCare. You see exactly where your instincts were right, where you underfunded a region that needed more, and where the real-world response fell short too. Funding gaps that took months of post-crisis analysis to uncover show up instantly.

There is also a global funding disparity view — a heatmap showing where relief money consistently falls short of estimated need, across every disaster in the dataset.

The goal is not to replace the people making these decisions. It is to make sure they have practiced before the real thing happens — with real data, real tradeoffs, and real consequences laid bare.
